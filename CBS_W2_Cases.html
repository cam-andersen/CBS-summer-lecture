The system bought high and sold low simultaneously across 154 stocks. Knight required an emergency bailout and was acquired four months later. It ceased to exist as an independent firm.

Pipeline Analysis

Knight Capital Group

  • One server missed the update. A technician failed to copy new code to one of eight servers. That server retained a dormant 2003 function called "Power Peg."
  • No deployment checklist. No procedure confirming all eight servers were identically configured before go-live.
  • No kill switch. Nothing halted trading when losses crossed any threshold.
  • 97 error messages — nobody's job. The system flagged the problem before markets opened. No one was watching.

Discussion

Knight Capital Group

97 error messages arrived before the market opened. What does it tell you about an organisation when warning signals exist but nobody's job it is to read them?

At which stage did this fail — data, training, deployment, or monitoring? Is it fair to call this an "AI failure" at all?

Knight had no kill switch. Should regulators require one for any automated system operating above a certain speed or scale?

What Happened

During the Lindt Café hostage siege in Sydney, thousands tried to leave the CBD simultaneously. The surge algorithm did exactly what it was designed to do. The in-app message read: "Demand is off the charts!" A human override made rides free within 40 minutes. All elevated fares were refunded.

Near-Miss Analysis

Uber Sydney

  • Uber already knew this risk. After Hurricane Sandy 2012, Uber committed to cap surge pricing during US disasters. That policy had not been extended to Australia.
  • What saved it: a human with override authority. Someone noticed, had authority, and acted in 40 minutes. No automatic trigger or monitoring alert existed.
  • Save type: partly structural, partly lucky. The authority existed. Whether the right person was available was luck. At 3am with a different team, the outcome could differ.

Discussion

Uber Sydney

The algorithm worked perfectly — it detected high demand and raised prices. The problem was context, not computation. Whose responsibility is it to define contexts where a working algorithm should not operate?

The rescue came from a human overriding the algorithm within 40 minutes. What conditions made that possible — and what if it had been 3am?

The fix that would have prevented Sydney already existed in New York. Had Sydney not happened, would that gap ever have been closed?

Air Canada Chatbot

The Chatbot That Hallucinated a Refund Policy

Sector

Aviation / customer service

Year

2022 (ruling Feb 2024)

Type

GenAI hallucination + legal liability

What Happened

Air Canada Chatbot

$812

ordered to pay

90 days

hallucinated policy

Feb 2024

tribunal ruling

1st

chatbot liability ruling

In November 2022, Jake Moffatt asked Air Canada's AI chatbot about bereavement fares after his grandmother died. The chatbot told him he could book now and apply for the bereavement discount within 90 days. This was false — the actual policy requires applying before travel. Moffatt booked, flew, and was denied the refund. Air Canada argued the chatbot was a "separate legal entity" not binding on the airline. The tribunal rejected this: "It should be obvious to Air Canada that it is responsible for all the information on its website. It makes no difference whether the information comes from a static page or a chatbot."

Why It Happened

Air Canada Chatbot

  • Next-token prediction treated as truth. The chatbot generated the most plausible completion, not the most accurate one. A 90-day refund window sounds reasonable — it just does not exist.
  • No retrieval layer. The chatbot was not connected to the actual refund policy document. It generated an answer from its training data, which included patterns about airline refund policies generally.
  • No validation gate. No mechanism checked the chatbot output against the real policy before presenting it to the customer.
  • Corporate liability denied, then enforced. Air Canada argued the chatbot was a "separate legal entity." The tribunal established: you are responsible for everything on your website.

Discussion

Air Canada Chatbot

The chatbot hallucinated a policy that does not exist. From Session 1: this is next-token prediction producing plausible but false output. Is there a difference between a chatbot that hallucinates and a human agent who gives wrong advice — and should there be?

Air Canada argued the chatbot was a "separate legal entity." The tribunal said it is part of the website. Where does corporate responsibility begin and end when an AI system speaks on your behalf? Should companies be held to a higher standard of accuracy for AI than for human agents?

The financial damage was small ($812). The precedent is enormous. What changes in how companies deploy customer-facing GenAI if every hallucination is legally binding?

Amazon Alexa

The Private Conversation That Was Sent Away

Sector

Consumer technology / voice assistants

Year

May 2018

Type

Composition of acceptable steps producing unacceptable outcome — save by luck

What Happened — A Four-Step Chain

Amazon Alexa

By 2018, Amazon Echo devices were installed in millions of homes, controlling lights, heating, and security systems. A Portland, Oregon family had wired their entire home with Echo devices. The system was working as designed.

  • Step 1: A word in background conversation sounded like "Alexa" — the device woke up.
  • Step 2: The subsequent conversation was interpreted as a "send message" request.
  • Step 3: Alexa asked aloud "To whom?" — and background conversation was interpreted as a contact name.
  • Step 4: Alexa asked aloud "[Contact name], right?" — and background conversation was interpreted as "right."
  • Result: A private conversation about hardwood floors was sent to the husband's employee in Seattle. Amazon confirmed the mechanism. Statement: "As unlikely as this string of events is, we are evaluating options."

Near-Miss Analysis

Amazon Alexa

  • What saved it: The employee who received the recording happened to be a known, trusted colleague who immediately called to warn the family.
  • Save type: Lucky. Had the recipient been unknown, malicious, or silent, this would have been an undetected, unremediated privacy violation.
  • The systemic question Amazon did not answer: How many partial chains occur across millions of devices without surfacing? The visible incident was the one where the last step completed.

Discussion

Amazon Alexa

The system worked correctly at every individual step. The failure emerged from the combination. What design principle should govern systems where individually acceptable steps can combine to produce unacceptable outcomes?

Amazon described the event as "extremely rare." Is rarity a sufficient governance defence for a privacy violation? How should probability of harm be weighed against severity of harm?

The save was not a governance feature — it was the identity of the person who received the recording. How should an organisation respond when a near-miss was averted by luck rather than by design?

Amazon AI Hiring Tool

Training Data That Taught the Machine to Discriminate

Sector

Technology / HR

Year

2014–2017 (reported 2018)

Type

Data governance — training data quality + stewardship

What Happened

Amazon AI Hiring Tool

In 2014, Amazon's HR team set out to build the "holy grail" of recruitment: an automated CV-screening tool. Feed the system 100 CVs, get back the top five candidates. Amazon had ten years of historical hiring decisions — CVs submitted and outcomes recorded — which appeared to be an ideal training set. A dedicated team built the tool over three years.

3 years

of development

2015

bias discovered

2017

scrapped

2018

publicly reported

By 2015 the team identified a serious problem: the model was systematically downgrading CVs from women for technical roles. It penalised CVs containing the word "women's" — as in "women's chess club" — and scored graduates of all-women's universities lower. When the team corrected for these specific signals, the model found other proxies for gender and continued to produce biased output. The team concluded it could not reliably verify gender-neutrality. Amazon scrapped the tool entirely in 2017. It was never used independently at scale. Reuters reported the story in 2018.

Governance Analysis

Amazon AI Hiring Tool

  • The stewardship question nobody asked. "Are our past hiring decisions a valid measure of future talent — or a record of whom we historically chose to hire?" Those are different questions. The team used the data without asking which question it actually answered.
  • Biased labels encoded as ground truth. Amazon's technical roles had been filled predominantly by men. The model correctly learned that successful hires looked mostly like men — and treated that as a signal of quality, not historical bias.
  • No quality gate at data ingestion. No audit of whether hired/not-hired labels were themselves unbiased. No diversity review of the training set before training began.
  • The AI was working exactly as designed. Learning from the data it was given, faithfully. That is the problem.

Discussion

Amazon AI Hiring Tool

The team framed the goal as "find candidates who look like our successful past hires." What is wrong with that framing? How would you reframe it?

The labels — hired/not hired — encoded a decade of the company's own bias. What process would you design to audit training labels before use? Who should run that audit — the technical team, HR, or an external body?

The AI was working exactly as designed — learning from the data it was given — and producing discriminatory outcomes. How does that change who we hold responsible?

Google Photos — "Gorillas"

The Fix That Was Not a Fix

Sector

Consumer technology / image recognition

Year

2015 (ongoing, unresolved)

Type

Training data bias — save by one public voice — governance response that created a second failure

What Happened

Google Photos

Google launched its Photos app in May 2015, featuring AI-powered automatic image tagging — grouping and labelling photos by subject matter. The training data for image recognition had not adequately represented darker skin tones. Google's workforce was approximately 2% Black at the time of launch.

2015

incident surfaced

2 hours

2025

0

On 29 June 2015, Jacky Alciné — a Brooklyn programmer of Haitian descent — found that multiple photos of him and a Black female friend had been labelled: "Gorillas." He posted on Twitter. Google's Chief Architect of Social responded within two hours. Google issued a public apology and took "immediate action." The immediate fix: remove the label "gorilla" from the entire system — including for actual gorillas. In 2018, Wired investigated: label still absent. In 2023, the New York Times confirmed the same. As of 2025, Google Photos cannot label gorillas, chimpanzees, baboons, orangutans, or monkeys. Apple Photos and Microsoft OneDrive adopted identical workarounds.

Near-Miss Analysis

Google Photos

  • What saved it (partially): One vocal user on the right platform at the right moment. Not a governance process — dependent on Alciné noticing, having the technical literacy to understand what happened, and choosing to post publicly.
  • What was not saved: The underlying bias. Google removed the label, not the problem. A decade later the workaround remains.
  • A second governance failure. Suppressing the label rather than repairing the model means darker skin tones are still underperforming in image recognition — we just cannot see where. Apple and Microsoft normalised the same workaround.
  • Alciné's diagnosis: "A diverse QA team could have caught this if they tested it on themselves."

Discussion

Google Photos

Alciné's tweet is the reason this case exists as a public record. If he had not posted it, what would have happened? What does this imply about organisations' ability to discover their own biases?

Google removed the label "gorilla" rather than fixing the underlying model. Is that a reasonable interim measure, a governance failure, or both? What would fixing the underlying model have required?

The same misclassification was almost certainly affecting other users who never reported it. What does it tell you about AI governance that the only failures organisations learn about are the ones that surface publicly? How should bias testing be designed to find what users don't report?

Starbucks — The Silent Failure as Near-Miss

How Close Is Close Enough to Count?

Sector

Retail / operations

Year

Sep 2025 – May 2026

Type

Silent distributed failure — ambiguous whether crisis was avoided or never materialised

What Happened

Starbucks — The Silent Failure

The same AI inventory system from Case F2. It generated silent errors across 11,000 stores for nine months. Visually similar products were misidentified. No monitoring framework aggregated or flagged the errors. In February 2026, Starbucks publicly stated the system had "improved inventory management." In May 2026 — three months later — the system was retired entirely. The cumulative operational impact was never publicly disclosed. The system was retired before the errors became a visible crisis — or the crisis never materialised. We do not know which.

Near-Miss Framework

Starbucks — The Silent Failure

  • This case has no confirmed moment of "almost catastrophe, then rescue." What exists is a system generating silent errors for nine months across 11,000 locations, retired before those errors were publicly quantified as a crisis.
  • The near-miss question the evidence cannot answer: How bad was it getting, and how close was it to becoming visible and material?
  • The other three near-misses were visible. Uber's surge sparked immediate public outrage. Alexa's recording was reported by the recipient. Alciné's tweet surfaced Google's bias. Starbucks's failure was invisible — no dramatic event, no single harmed party, no monitoring alert.
  • We do not know. And that is the point. The uncertainty is itself the governance lesson.

Discussion

Starbucks — The Silent Failure

Knight Capital lost $440M in 45 minutes — loud, immediate, impossible to ignore. Starbucks's failure was quiet, distributed, and accumulated over nine months. Which failure mode is harder to govern — and which is more likely to go unaddressed for longer?

The cumulative inventory impact across 11,000 stores over nine months was never disclosed. Does the absence of disclosed harm mean the harm was small — or that it was never measured? How should that uncertainty affect your assessment?

Is this a near-miss or just a slow failure? What criteria would you use to distinguish the two — and does the distinction matter for how we govern similar deployments?

Dutch Childcare Benefits

An Algorithm Brought Down a Government

Sector

Public sector / welfare administration

Year

2013–2019 (scandal broke 2020–2021)

Type

Data governance — all five warning signs simultaneously

What Happened

Dutch Childcare Benefits

In 2012, the Dutch government came under political pressure to reduce welfare fraud. The childcare benefits unit was given an expanded budget with an explicit condition: demonstrate fraud reduction. In 2013 it deployed an algorithmic risk-scoring system to identify benefit claims warranting investigation. The goal appeared reasonable — use data to target enforcement resources efficiently.

70%

2021

5

Between 2013 and 2019, approximately 26,000 families were wrongly accused of childcare benefits fraud. Nearly 70% were first- or second-generation migrants. Families were required to repay full benefit entitlements — sometimes tens of thousands of euros — with no explanation and no route to contest the decision. Children were taken into state custody where parents could not repay. Families lost homes, lost jobs. A Dutch parliamentary inquiry described it as "unprecedented injustice." In January 2021, the entire Dutch cabinet resigned.

Governance Analysis — Five Simultaneous Failures

Dutch Childcare Benefits

  • Illegal feature variable. Nationality used as a risk signal. The Dutch DPA confirmed this was illegal. The correlation existed because enforcement had always targeted immigrant communities — the model learned and amplified existing bias.
  • Perverse institutional incentive. The unit's budget was conditional on demonstrating fraud reduction. False positives had institutional reward; the cost of being wrong in the other direction was not designed for.
  • Automation bias eliminated human judgement. Caseworkers accepted algorithm output as a final verdict, not one signal among many.
  • No transparency, no contestability. Families received no explanation and had no route to challenge — violating basic administrative justice and GDPR.
  • Opaque model, no lineage. Officially described as a "black box self-learning system." Risk indicators not documented for external scrutiny.

Discussion

Dutch Childcare Benefits

"Use data to target enforcement resources efficiently" sounds sensible. At what point does an efficiency goal become a rights violation? Who in the organisation should be asking that question before deployment?

Caseworkers were supposed to exercise judgement but rubber-stamped the algorithm. Whose fault is that — the caseworkers, the managers, or the technologists? How do you design systems that preserve genuine human oversight rather than performative oversight?

26,000 families were wrongly accused. Many have not yet received full compensation. What does this tell you about the asymmetry between the speed at which algorithmic harm can be inflicted and the speed at which it can be remediated?

What Happened

A finance employee at Arup's Hong Kong office received an email from the "CFO" requesting urgent confidential transfers. The employee was suspicious — until a video call confirmed it. On the call: the CFO and several colleagues, all looking and sounding exactly right. Every person on the call was an AI-generated deepfake, built from publicly available video and audio of Arup executives scraped from LinkedIn, YouTube, and corporate recordings. The employee made 15 transfers totalling $25.6 million to five bank accounts. The fraud was discovered only through a routine post-transaction follow-up with actual headquarters. Why It Happened

Arup Deepfake

  • Video as verification is dead. "I saw them on camera" was the gold standard for identity confirmation. GenAI deepfakes have made it worthless.
  • Training data is public. Every executive with a LinkedIn profile, a conference talk, or an earnings call is training data for their own impersonation.
  • Siloed verification. A finance employee in Hong Kong made decisions based on instructions from what appeared to be London headquarters. No out-of-band verification protocol existed.
  • AI as weapon, not tool. The AI did not malfunction. It worked exactly as the attackers intended. This is a different governance problem entirely.

Discussion

Arup Deepfake

"I spoke to them on video" has been the gold standard of identity verification for decades. Generative AI has made that worthless. What replaces it? What verification procedure would have caught this?

The deepfakes were built from publicly available footage. Every executive with a LinkedIn profile, a conference talk, or an earnings call is now training data for their own impersonation. Is this a governance problem for individuals, for organisations, or for the platforms that host the footage?

This is generative AI used as a weapon, not a tool. The AI did not malfunction — it worked exactly as the attackers intended. How does this change the governance conversation from "how do we make AI safe" to "how do we defend against AI being used against us"?

What Happened

IBM Watson for Oncology

In 2012, fresh from its celebrated Jeopardy! victory, IBM announced Watson as the future of medicine. IBM partnered with MD Anderson Cancer Center to build the "Oncology Expert Advisor" — an AI system that would read patient records, process oncology literature, and recommend cancer treatment plans. The pitch: democratise the expertise of the world's best cancer doctors for community hospitals everywhere. The contract was initially scoped at six months and $2.4 million.

The project ran for five years. The contract was extended twelve times. IBM was paid $39.2 million; PwC a further $23 million in consulting fees. Total: approximately $62 million. The system never entered clinical use with a single patient. Internal IBM documents showed Watson generated treatment recommendations physicians described as "unsafe and incorrect." By 2018, more than a dozen hospital partners had reduced or ended Watson Oncology projects. IBM sold Watson Health entirely in 2022.

Pipeline Analysis

IBM Watson for Oncology

  • Problem misdiagnosed at ideation. Watson's capability was text retrieval — what Jeopardy! required. Clinical oncology requires reasoning under uncertainty with incomplete, evolving, high-stakes information. These are fundamentally different cognitive tasks. Never validated before public announcement.
  • Synthetic training data. Watson was not trained on real patient records. Physicians at one institution wrote hypothetical scenarios. The model learned the preferences of a small group, then encountered the full diversity of real patients.
  • No deployment target. Designed for community oncologists — but no agreements with community hospitals were ever signed before spending began.
  • Two actors, two incentives. IBM needed prestigious press. MD Anderson needed a fundable vision. Neither incentive required the technology to actually work.

Discussion

IBM Watson for Oncology

"Democratise access to the world's best cancer doctors" is a compelling mission. At what point does a compelling mission become a reason to skip validation? Who should be asking the hard questions when the vision is this attractive?

The project ran five years and cost $62M without producing a deployable system. At what point should someone have stopped it — and who had the authority?

Two organisations — IBM and MD Anderson — each had incentives that did not require the system to actually work. What governance structure should exist when two parties each benefit from continuing a failing project?

Pieces Technologies

The Healthcare AI That Overstated Its Own Accuracy

Sector

Healthcare / clinical AI

Year

2024 (settlement Sep 2024)

Type

GenAI hallucination + misrepresented metrics

What Happened

Pieces Technologies

Pieces Technologies built a generative AI product for hospitals that summarised patient charts and drafted clinical notes. At least four major Texas hospitals provided their patients' real-time healthcare data to Pieces. The company marketed a "severe hallucination rate" of less than 0.001% — less than 1 per 100,000 outputs. The Texas Attorney General investigated and found these metrics were "likely inaccurate" and may have deceived hospitals about the safety of the product. In September 2024, Pieces entered a first-of-its-kind settlement — the first enforcement action against a generative AI company for misrepresenting hallucination rates. The five-year agreement requires clear disclosures in all marketing and advertising.

Why It Happened

Pieces Technologies

  • Self-reported accuracy with no industry standard. No agreed methodology for measuring hallucination rates in clinical AI. Pieces built its own benchmark and marketed the result.
  • Proxy creep. A hallucination rate became a proxy for safety. The metric was optimised; patient safety was not directly measured.
  • Real patient data, unvalidated outputs. Four hospitals fed live patient records into a system whose accuracy claims were later found to be likely inaccurate.
  • First enforcement action. The Texas AG settlement established that misrepresenting AI accuracy is a regulatory violation, not just a marketing problem.

Discussion

Pieces Technologies

Compare this to IBM Watson. Both failed in healthcare. Watson never reached patients. Pieces was deployed in four hospitals using real patient data. Which failure is worse — the one that never worked, or the one that worked but overstated its accuracy?

There is no industry standard for measuring hallucination rates in clinical AI. Pieces built its own methodology and then marketed the results. Who should set that standard — the companies, the hospitals, or a regulator?

From Session 1: a generative AI system predicts the next most probable token. In a clinical note, the most probable completion might be medically plausible but factually wrong for this specific patient. How should hospitals verify AI-generated clinical documentation?

On 2 October 2023, a hit-and-run driver struck a pedestrian in San Francisco, throwing her into the path of a Cruise autonomous vehicle. The robotaxi braked but ran her over. Then it executed a "pullover" manoeuvre — dragging her 20 feet at 7 mph. The next morning, Cruise showed regulators an edited video that ended before the dragging. Why It Happened

Cruise Robotaxi

  • Scale before data. Cruise expanded faster than Waymo with less accumulated driving data. The speed-to-market incentive overrode the safety-through-patience approach.
  • Deliberate cover-up. The edited video and incomplete NHTSA report were human decisions, not algorithmic failures. The governance failure was organisational, not technical.
  • Two failures, one incident. The AI failure (dragging) and the corporate failure (hiding it) are separable but the second destroyed the trust needed to recover from the first.

Discussion

Cruise Robotaxi

The initial collision was caused by a human hit-and-run driver. The autonomous vehicle was the second actor. But the cover-up was entirely human. Which failure is the AI failure and which is the organisational failure? Can you separate them?

From Session 1: an agentic system follows a plan-act-observe loop. The Cruise vehicle observed a collision, acted by braking, then followed its pullover protocol without re-evaluating the situation. What does this tell you about the limits of agentic systems in edge cases?

Waymo accumulated 200 million miles before scaling. Cruise expanded faster with less data. Compare the two pipeline illustrations. Is there a point at which regulatory patience with autonomous systems should run out?

Sector

Type

What Happened

Facebook / Cambridge Analytica

Facebook's pre-2014 API allowed third-party apps to collect data not only from users who installed them, but from all of those users' Facebook friends — without the friends' knowledge or consent. This was a deliberate design choice. Aleksandr Kogan, a data scientist, built a Facebook personality quiz app in 2013, paying approximately 270,000 users to complete it. Through friends-of-friends harvesting, his firm collected data from up to 87 million Facebook profiles. Kogan passed this data to Cambridge Analytica, which used it for micro-targeted political advertising in the 2016 US presidential campaigns.

Facebook learned of the misuse in 2015 — reported by The Guardian. Facebook asked Cambridge Analytica to delete the data. Cambridge Analytica said it had. Facebook took their word for it, performed no verification, and did not inform the 87 million affected users. In March 2018, whistleblower Christopher Wylie provided documentation to The Guardian and The New York Times. Cambridge Analytica declared bankruptcy in May 2018. Consequences: FTC $5 billion fine on Facebook · ICO £500,000 (legal maximum, pre-GDPR) · had GDPR applied: potentially ~$2.3 billion.

Governance Analysis

Facebook / Cambridge Analytica

  • Failure layer 1 — Platform design. Friends-of-friends data harvesting was a deliberate feature. The governance question — what happens when that data is used for purposes users never imagined — was not asked.
  • Failure layer 2 — Facebook's 2015 non-response. Discovery without disclosure. No audit, no technical verification, no notification to 87 million affected users. Three years between discovery and public exposure.
  • Purpose limitation violation. Data collected for academic personality research. Used for commercial political micro-targeting. Consent framework entirely misaligned with actual use.
  • Ownership question, unanswered. Whose data was it — the user's, the friend's, Facebook's, Kogan's, or Cambridge Analytica's? Each step individually defensible; aggregate outcome a large-scale privacy violation.

Discussion

Facebook / Cambridge Analytica

There are three actors — Facebook, Kogan/GSR, and Cambridge Analytica — each of whom can point to someone else's decision as the root cause. Who bears primary responsibility? Does your answer change depending on which governance failure you're analysing?

The data was collected for academic research and used for political micro-targeting. What technical or contractual mechanism should have prevented it — and at which point in the chain?

The UK fine was £500,000 — the legal maximum — because the incident predated GDPR. GDPR was partly designed in response to cases like this. Does that make Cambridge Analytica a governance success (regulation improved) or a governance failure (regulation was too late)? Can both be true simultaneously?

Pipeline Illustration — Data Quality + Pipeline Discipline

Stripe Radar

Data quality · Clean labelled data · Continuous retraining · Feedback loop governance
  • Every transaction Stripe processes is either fraud or not — ground truth is built in. No labelling problem.
  • Pipeline designed correctly from day one: expert labels, continuous retraining as fraud patterns evolve, real-time inference on every transaction.
  • Fraud detection on large users: 59% → 97% when transformer-style modelling applied. Card-testing fraud dropped 64%.
  • Key lesson: Narrow, well-defined problem with inherently accurate labels. System designed to fail safe — declined transaction beats fraudulent charge.

Use as contrast with

Knight Capital (deployment failure, no monitoring)IBM Watson (training data failure)Starbucks (no monitoring)

Pipeline Illustration — Training Data Quality + Validation Rigour

DeepMind AlphaFold

Data quality over quantity · Expert-validated training data · Holdout validation design · Open release as governance
  • Training corpus: ~170,000 structures from the Protein Data Bank — tiny by commercial AI standards, but every entry peer-reviewed and experimentally validated over 50 years.
  • Validation: the CASP competition tests predictions on newly determined proteins no team has seen — the most rigorous possible holdout. AlphaFold won by a margin that stunned the field.
  • 3 million researchers in 190 countries now use it freely — open release as a deliberate governance decision.
  • Key lesson: Quality over quantity in training data. Problem reformulated at conceptual level before model was built.

Use as contrast with

IBM Watson (synthetic training data, no rigorous validation)Amazon Hiring (biased labels, no audit)

Pipeline Illustration — Scoping + Human Oversight

JP Morgan COIN

Narrow problem scoping · Expert-annotated training data · Verifiable ground truth · Human oversight preserved
  • Processed 12,000 commercial credit agreements in seconds — work previously requiring 360,000 hours of legal time annually — with lower error rates than human reviewers.
  • Governance decisions: extremely narrow problem scope; training data annotated by domain experts; every output directly verifiable against the source document.
  • Unusual cases routed to human review. One document type first, validated, then extended. Incremental deployment — not a big bang.
  • Key lesson: Ambitious AI built on boring governance foundations. The narrower the scope, the easier the validation.

Use as contrast with

IBM Watson (ambiguous scope, synthetic data)Dutch Benefits (no human oversight, automation bias)

Pipeline Illustration — Incremental Deployment + Data Accumulation

Waymo One

Staged deployment · Real-world data over time · Redundant safety architecture · Patience before scale
  • 200 million autonomous miles on public roads before reaching 500,000 paid rides per week in 2025–2026. Phoenix first, then San Francisco, then expansion.
  • Sensor stack (LiDAR + radar + cameras) encodes a deliberate governance value: fail safe over cost efficiency.
  • Cruise expanded faster with less data — suspended after a serious incident in 2023. Waymo's patience is the differentiator.
  • Key lesson: Scale is earned by data accumulation, not announced on a product roadmap. Transparency reports as a governance commitment.

Use as contrast with

Knight Capital (no monitoring, no circuit breakers)Starbucks (no monitoring framework)IBM Watson (marketing ahead of validation)
