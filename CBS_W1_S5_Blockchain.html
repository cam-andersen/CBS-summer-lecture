Meet the entities in every transaction

No banks. No head office. These are the only players that matter on a blockchain.

👤
You

Decide to send value and authorise the transaction.

👛
Your Wallet

Prepares and cryptographically signs the transaction.

🖥️
Nodes

Validate and propagate transactions. No single owner.

⛏️
Miners

Bundle transactions into blocks. Compete to earn fees.

🌐
The Network

Collective of all nodes. Nobody owns it. No off switch.

Note: a wallet is not a node, and a miner is a specialised node. These roles are distinct — and understanding who does what is the foundation of everything that follows.

Step 0 — Your wallet: a key, not an account

Before you send anything, you need a wallet. It works nothing like a bank account.

🔑
Private Key
Your secret. Never shared. Whoever holds this key controls the funds — no password reset, no bank to call.
Lose it = lose everything permanently
🏠
Public Key / Address
Your address on the network. Safe to share — like giving someone your bank account number so they can send you money.
Anyone can send to it. Nobody can spend from it.
🏦
vs. A Bank Account
A bank holds your money and lets you access it. A wallet holds your key — the network holds the record. No intermediary between you and your funds.
Sovereignty — and full responsibility
The bank does not go down. There is no bank. You are the bank.

Steps 1 & 2 — Sign and broadcast

Your wallet does two things when you hit Send: builds the transaction and broadcasts it

Step 1 — Build & Sign
Recipient address
Where the value goes — the receiver's public address
Amount
Exact value to send, denominated in the network token
Fee
Your bid for miner attention. Higher fee = faster inclusion. You choose the trade-off.
Digital signature
Your private key signs the whole thing. Proves you authorised it — without revealing your key.
Step 2 — Broadcast
Your wallet sends the signed transaction to one connected node. That node gossips it to its neighbours. Within seconds it has spread across the entire network.
You → Node A → B, C → D, E, F → …
No single path. No single point of failure. The gossip protocol ensures every node receives it.
At this point — no miner has touched it yet. It is just a signed message floating in the network.

Steps 3 & 4 — Nodes validate, then it waits

Every node checks independently. Then your transaction joins a queue.

Step 3 — Validation (every node)
✓ Signature valid? Genuinely from the sender
✓ Enough balance? Funds actually exist
✓ No double-spend? Same funds not sent twice
✓ Fee sufficient? Worth a miner's attention
No single authority decides. Every node runs these checks independently. Pass all four — or get rejected.
Step 4 — The Mempool (waiting room)
Valid transactions enter the mempool — a pool of unconfirmed transactions waiting to be included in a block.
The queue
Higher fee → miners pick you first → faster confirmation. Lower fee → you wait. During congestion, transactions can wait hours.
Think of it as a taxi rank in peak hour. The cab (miner) picks the highest bidder.

Setting up the race — how miners build their candidate block

Before the race starts, every miner independently assembles their entry. No coordination. No starting gun.

①
Previous block sealed
The moment a miner wins the previous round, every other miner stops what they were doing. The old race is over. A new one begins immediately — no pause, no announcement.
②
Each miner selects transactions from their mempool
Independently and simultaneously. Each miner picks whichever transactions they want — typically highest fee first. Two miners might build slightly different candidate blocks from the same mempool.
③
Miner adds their own reward transaction
A special coinbase transaction: "if I win, pay me 3.125 BTC plus all the fees in this block." This is self-declared — only valid if the miner actually wins the puzzle.
④
Race starts — simultaneously, globally
With candidate block assembled, each miner starts hashing. Trillions of attempts per second. The puzzle: find a number (nonce) that makes the block's hash start with enough zeros. Pure random search.

Step 5 — Mining: the race to seal the block

Thousands of miners compete simultaneously. First to solve the puzzle wins.

Back in 10 minutes: how the winning block spreads, how the network agrees, and what finality actually means.

Steps 6 & 7 — Broadcast the block, reach consensus

The winning miner shares their block. Every other node checks it. The network agrees.

Step 6 — Block broadcast
The winning miner gossips the sealed block across the network — same mechanism as transaction broadcast. Within seconds every node has it.
Other miners immediately stop working on the old puzzle and start competing for the next block. The chain always moves forward.
Step 7 — Consensus
Every node independently validates the new block. If it is valid, they add it to their chain. If not, they reject it.
✓ Valid transactions inside?
✓ Correct previous hash? Links to the chain?
✓ Puzzle solved correctly? Work was done?
No voting. No committee. Pure rule-following by every node independently.

Steps 8–11 — Settlement and finality

Once consensus is reached, four things happen simultaneously across every node on earth

📤
Sender balance decreases
Amount sent plus the fee, deducted from the sender's address. Instantly reflected in every node's copy of the ledger.
📥
Receiver balance increases
Exact amount received. No clearing delay. No correspondent bank. No T+2. The ledger updates the moment consensus is reached.
⛏️
Miner collects the fee
All transaction fees in the block go to the winning miner, plus the block reward. The economic engine that sustains the network.
🔒
Finality — it is permanent
Each new block added on top makes reversal exponentially harder. After 6 blocks (~60 min for Bitcoin) — it is considered final. No chargeback. No reversal. No dispute.
Compare to T+2: funds confirmed in minutes, not days. No intermediary. No counterparty risk.

Start here: what is a transaction?

The atomic unit of blockchain. Everything else is built on top of this.

Next: transactions are bundled into a block

New vocabulary introduced one term at a time

HASH
A mathematical fingerprint of the block's entire contents. Change any single byte and the hash completely changes. Impossible to reverse-engineer.
NONCE
A number the miner adjusts trillions of times until the block's hash starts with enough zeros. This is the work in proof-of-work — computationally expensive, trivial to verify.
MINER
A node that competes to seal blocks. Whoever solves the nonce puzzle first adds the block and earns the reward. Anyone can be a miner — this is the decentralisation mechanism.
BLOCK REWARD
New Bitcoin created and awarded to the winning miner. This is how Bitcoin enters circulation — and why miners participate. No central bank. No treasurer. Code issues the reward automatically.

Finally: blocks are chained together

One new term — prev hash — makes the whole structure immutable

Types of blockchain

Not all blockchains are Bitcoin. The relevant question for finance: who can participate?

Public / Permissionless
Anyone can join, read, write, validate
No central authority. Maximum decentralisation. All transactions visible. Slow (~7 tx/sec Bitcoin vs ~1,700 Visa).
Bitcoin est. 2009
Digital gold, store of value, peer-to-peer payments. $1T+ market cap. Used by MicroStrategy, El Salvador as legal tender.
Ethereum est. 2015
Smart contracts, DeFi, NFTs, tokenised assets. BlackRock BUIDL fund runs on Ethereum. ~$500B market cap.
Solana est. 2020
High-speed public chain (~65,000 tx/sec). Used by Visa for USDC settlement pilots. Payments and consumer apps.
Private / Permissioned
Known participants — invitation required
Faster (1,000s tx/sec). Privacy controls. Regulators know who participates. Most financial deployments use this model.
Hyperledger Fabric Linux Foundation
Enterprise supply chain and trade finance. Used by Maersk (TradeLens), HSBC for FX settlement, Walmart food tracing.
R3 Corda est. 2016
Purpose-built for financial services. Used by DTCC, Goldman Sachs, HSBC for bond issuance and repo settlement.
Quorum (JPMorgan) est. 2016
JPMorgan's private Ethereum fork. Powers JPM Coin for intraday dollar transfers between institutional clients.
Consortium & DAG
Shared governance across competitors
Consortium: group of industry peers run the nodes jointly. DAG: no blocks — transactions validate each other directly.
SWIFT GPI / ISO 20022 consortium
11,000+ banks share a messaging layer. Not a blockchain but the consortium model — shared governance, competitive participants.
Hedera Hashgraph DAG · est. 2019
Governed by Google, IBM, Boeing, Deutsche Telekom. Used by Standard Bank for cross-border payments in Africa.
Canton Network est. 2023
Goldman Sachs, BNP Paribas, Deloitte. Privacy-preserving interoperability for tokenised assets across institutions.

How traditional banks are adapting — and what has failed

Banks take the efficiency gains, strip out the parts that don't work for them

① Build their own chain
Take the technology, keep full control
JPMorgan Onyx / JPM Coin — intraday institutional transfers. Goldman GS DAP — tokenised asset platform. HSBC FX Everywhere — internal FX netting across 27 currencies.
② Join industry consortia
Shared infrastructure, shared governance
Canton Network (Goldman, BNP, Deloitte). R3 Corda network (HSBC, DTCC, Barclays). SWIFT blockchain interoperability pilots. Logic: no bank builds alone, no bank lets a competitor go first.
③ Tokenise on existing chains
Use public infrastructure for regulated products
BlackRock BUIDL on Ethereum ($500M+). Franklin Templeton money market fund on Polygon. Société Générale covered bond on Ethereum. They don't build — they deploy.
What has failed
TradeLens (Maersk + IBM) shut down 2022
Global trade documentation on Hyperledger. Shut after 4 years — competitors refused to join a platform controlled by a rival (Maersk). Governance, not technology, killed it.
ASX CHESS Replacement cancelled 2023
7-year project to replace Australia's equity settlement system with blockchain. Cancelled after $170M spent. Complexity and scope far exceeded blockchain's readiness for the task.
We.Trade (European banks) collapsed 2023
12-bank consortium for trade finance on Hyperledger. Collapsed after banks lost interest — insufficient transaction volumes, high operating costs, no network effects.
What has worked
JPM Coin — $1B+ daily volume in institutional transfers · HSBC FX Everywhere — $2.5T in FX netting since 2018 · BlackRock BUIDL — $500M+ tokenised treasury fund · Contour — live trade finance letters of credit in 100+ countries
Pattern: narrow scope, clear efficiency gain, controlled participant set. Broad consortia with competitive participants and unclear governance models consistently fail.

Where blockchain is actually used in finance

Separate the hype from the production deployments

✓ Working in production
Cross-border payments
RippleNet used by Santander, SBI Holdings. Reduces correspondent banking steps from 3-4 days to seconds.
Trade finance
Letters of credit on blockchain (Contour, formerly Voltron). Reduces processing from 5-10 days to 24 hours.
Tokenised assets
BlackRock BUIDL fund ($500M+ AUM), Franklin Templeton money market — real assets on public blockchain.
Central Bank Digital Currencies
50+ countries piloting or launched. China e-CNY, ECB digital euro in development. Most use permissioned blockchain.
✗ Where it has not delivered
Supply chain provenance
Walmart food safety, Maersk TradeLens (shut down 2022). The data going on-chain is still entered by humans — blockchain doesn't solve the garbage-in problem.
Most retail banking
Traditional databases are 100x faster and far cheaper to operate. Blockchain only wins where you need to remove the trusted intermediary — most banks are happy to be that intermediary.
DeFi at scale
Genuine innovation in protocol design but $3.8B lost to hacks in 2022 alone. Smart contract bugs are catastrophic — code is law, including the bugs.

Future outlook

Three threads, three different timelines

Near-term (1–3 years)
Tokenisation of real-world assets
Bonds, equities, real estate, private credit on-chain. BlackRock, Fidelity, Franklin Templeton already live. Larry Fink: "tokenisation of financial assets is the next generation for markets."
Expected: $10–16 trillion in tokenised assets by 2030 (McKinsey, BCG)
Medium-term (3–7 years)
CBDCs and programmable money
Central bank digital currencies move from pilot to live. Programmable money — automatic tax collection, conditional payments, expiring stimulus. The monetary system becomes software.
134 countries representing 98% of global GDP exploring CBDCs (Atlantic Council, 2024)
Long-term (7+ years)
Convergence with AI and quantum
AI agents transacting autonomously on blockchain rails. Quantum-resistant cryptography baked into new chains. The trust infrastructure of a future where machines transact with machines.
Speculative — but the S5 synthesis graph connects all three

Blockchain is not replacing the financial system. It is becoming part of the infrastructure of the financial system — especially where trust between institutions needs to be provable rather than assumed.
