Data classification — what tier is it?

Before you govern data you must classify it. Tier determines handling, storage, access, and retention.

🔴
Tier 1 — Restricted
Highest sensitivity
Personal data, passwords, health records, financial details, legal privileged
Encryption at rest + in transit. Need-to-know access only. Breach notification required.
🟡
Tier 2 — Confidential
Internal sensitive
Business strategy, salary data, client contracts, M&A activity, model weights
Encrypted. Role-based access. Not shared externally without NDA.
🔵
Tier 3 — Internal
General internal use
Internal reports, meeting notes, project documentation, aggregated stats
Standard access controls. Employees only. No special encryption required.
🟢
Tier 4 — Public
No restrictions
Press releases, published research, marketing materials, open datasets
Freely shareable. No access controls needed. Still verify accuracy.

The question that determines tier: what is the impact if this data is disclosed, modified, or lost without authorisation?

The ownership question

Before any governance framework, policy, or tool, there is one question that determines everything:
Who is responsible for this data?

In theory

  • Business owns the data
  • IT manages the infrastructure
  • Roles are clearly defined

In practice

  • Ownership sits with whoever built the system
  • Nobody maintains quality when that person leaves
  • Regulators get no answer because no one is accountable

Governance requirements by paradigm

Traceability
✓ Training data documented and static — full lineage possible
✗ Full lineage — web-scale corpus, sources often unclear or unlicensed
Explainability
✓ Feature importance, SHAP values — why this output, mathematically
✗ Per-decision explanation — probabilistic outputs resist full explanation
Reproducibility
✓ Same input → same output. Audit trail is deterministic.
✗ Deterministic audit trail — same prompt → different outputs each time
Data quality
✓ Controlled dataset, validated and documented before training
~ Quality is uneven and assumed. You govern the use, not the corpus.
Output monitoring
— Model outputs are static. Monitor for distribution shift, not live outputs.
★ New requirement: live output sampling, hallucination detection, content filtering — every response is different
Prompt governance
— Does not exist in traditional ML. Inputs are structured data, not natural language.
★ New requirement: who can prompt? What prompts are logged? System prompts are policy documents.
✓ Traditional handles this ✗ Traditional approach breaks down ★ New requirement — did not exist before ~ Partially applicable

The gap

Regulation assumes capabilities that GenAI cannot fully deliver

What regulators want

  • Documented training data
  • Explainable decisions
  • Reproducible outputs
  • Human oversight
THE GAP

What GenAI can deliver

  • Approximate provenance
  • Post-hoc reasoning (sometimes)
  • Stochastic — not reproducible
  • Human oversight is hard to scale
GenAI initiatives are being delayed or blocked — not for technical reasons, but because compliance frameworks don't know how to handle them.

Model drift — when the world moves and the model doesn't

A model that was accurate at deployment degrades silently as the world changes around it

Data drift
The statistical distribution of inputs changes. A fraud model trained on 2022 transaction patterns sees 2025 transaction patterns — different amounts, channels, merchant categories.
The data changed. The model didn't know.
Concept drift
The relationship between inputs and the target variable changes. What "creditworthy" means shifts after a recession. The model learned the old relationship and is now optimising for the wrong thing.
The world changed. The model didn't know.
Model degradation
Even without distribution shift, model performance erodes as edge cases accumulate. The training set didn't cover everything. Over time the gaps compound.
Reality is more complex than the training set.
The confidence trap
The model does not know it has drifted. It continues producing high-confidence outputs — because it is still making internally consistent decisions within its training distribution.
High confidence ≠ high accuracy.

Model drift — what it looks like over time

Performance degrades silently — often nobody notices until a decision causes visible harm

Governance response
Set performance thresholds. Automated alerts when accuracy drops below SLA.
Retraining trigger
When drift detected: retrain on recent data, validate, redeploy. Not ad hoc — policy-driven.
The governance gap
Most organisations have no drift monitoring. They find out when a decision fails publicly.

Where governance is right now

What regulation now requires

Click any item to expand

GDPR
Data accuracy & purpose limitation
Personal data must be accurate, up to date, and collected only for stated purposes. Organisations must demonstrate compliance on request.
GDPR
Privacy by design & default
Systems must be designed with privacy protections built in from the start. Data minimisation and access controls are required by architecture.
EU AI Act
Data quality & traceability for high-risk AI
AI systems that affect people's lives must document training data quality, lineage, and testing methodology. High-risk categories include credit, employment, and healthcare.
EU AI Act
Human oversight & explainability
Organisations must explain how a model made a decision and maintain meaningful human oversight of consequential automated decisions.
Gap
Most organisations aren't ready
These requirements assume governance is already in place. For many organisations, the regulation arrived before the governance infrastructure did.

The global regulatory patchwork

The EU leads with binding law. Everyone else is watching — and some are doing the opposite.

🇪🇺 EU
Binding law, risk tiers
GDPR (2018) + EU AI Act (2024). Highest-risk AI banned. High-risk requires audit, explainability, human oversight. Fines up to 7% of global revenue.
Approach: precautionary · rights-based
🇺🇸 US
Sectoral, voluntary
No federal AI law. NIST AI Risk Management Framework (voluntary). State-level patchwork: Illinois BIPA, California CPRA. Biden EO (2023) reversed by Trump (2025).
Approach: innovation-first · light-touch
🇬🇧 UK
Pro-innovation, principles
Deliberately chose NOT to pass an AI Act. Sector-led regulation (FCA, ICO, CMA). AI Safety Institute (2023) for frontier models. Post-Brexit divergence from EU intentional.
Approach: principles-based · sector-led
🇨🇳 China
Targeted rules, state priority
Algorithm recommendation rules (2022), generative AI rules (2023). Requires labelling of AI content. State security exemptions. Data localisation mandated.
Approach: targeted · sovereignty-first

As a CBS graduate operating in global finance: GDPR applies to any data about EU residents, anywhere. Your US counterpart faces none of these requirements — yet.

When governance fails — three cases

Not algorithm failures. Data governance failures. The distinction matters.

CASE 1 · 2014–2018
Amazon Hiring Algorithm
Built on 10 years of hiring data. The data reflected a decade of male-dominated hiring. The model learned to penalise CVs containing the word "women's" (as in "women's chess club"). Systematically downgraded female candidates.
What failed:
No audit of training data for historical bias. No monitoring of outputs by demographic. Discovered internally — never deployed, but built for 4 years.
CASE 2 · 2019
Dutch Child Benefits (SyRI)
Dutch tax authority used algorithmic risk profiling to detect benefit fraud. Flagged 26,000 families — disproportionately ethnic minorities and dual nationals. Families had benefits clawed back, some forced into bankruptcy. Court ruled it violated human rights in 2020.
What failed:
No explainability. No right of appeal. Nationality used as a risk factor. Zero human oversight of automated decisions.
CASE 3 · 2020
UK A-Level Grades (Ofqual)
Exams cancelled due to COVID. Algorithm predicted grades using school historical performance. Systematically downgraded students from lower-performing schools — even when their predicted teacher grades were higher. 39% of grades lowered. Disproportionate impact on state schools.
What failed:
School-level data used to predict individual outcomes. No override mechanism. No pre-deployment equity audit. Reversed within days under public pressure.

Each could have been caught with basic governance: data audits, demographic impact testing, human oversight, explainability requirements

The tension nobody has resolved

What frameworks assume

  • Deterministic: same input → same output
  • Full traceability of training data
  • Explainable, auditable decisions
  • Stable models that don't change without notice

What GenAI actually is

  • Probabilistic: same prompt → different outputs
  • Web-scale data of unclear provenance
  • Decisions emerge from billions of parameters
  • Models update continuously; prompts change behaviour

The right to erasure — Article 17 GDPR

A person can ask you to delete their data. What does that mean when their data trained your model?

What the law says
A data subject can request erasure of their personal data if it is no longer necessary for the purpose it was collected, they withdraw consent, or they object to processing.
For a database: delete the row. ✓
For a model trained on that row: no clear answer exists.
The problem
Training data is baked in
A model's weights encode patterns from every training example. You cannot "remove" one person's contribution without retraining.
Retraining is expensive
Frontier model training costs $50M–$100M. Even enterprise fine-tuning costs thousands. One erasure request cannot trigger a full retrain.
Regulators haven't decided
GDPR Article 17 predates LLMs. Whether a trained model "contains" personal data is legally unresolved in 2025.

Right to erasure — practical implications for AI teams

What you actually do when a request arrives — and what governance decisions to make before one does

Option 1 — Delete & retrain
Remove the data from training set and retrain from scratch. Technically clean. Practically impossible at scale.
Viable for: small models, high-stakes regulated use cases
Option 2 — Machine unlearning
Emerging technique: selectively remove a data point's influence without full retraining. Active research area — not production-ready at scale yet.
Viable for: future compliance tooling — watch this space
Option 3 — Prevention by design
Only train on data where you have solid legal basis. Anonymise or pseudonymise before training. Keep detailed data lineage so you can identify what to remove.
The only currently scalable answer

The governance decision to make before training: can we demonstrate a legal basis for every data point? If not, don't train on it.

The tension: GDPR's right to erasure assumes data lives in a database. AI makes data live in weights. The law has not caught up.

What organisations are trying

Click to explore — emerging approaches, not yet proven

Emerging

Tiered risk frameworks for different AI types

Deterministic ML gets full explainability requirements; GenAI gets alternative controls — output monitoring, human review, use-case restrictions.

Active

Separate governance tracks for GenAI vs. traditional ML

Traditional ML goes through established review boards; GenAI use cases go through a fast-track with different criteria — focusing on output risk.

Emerging

Proxy explainability tools for probabilistic systems

Tools that provide partial answers: which inputs influenced output, what topics the model considered, where confidence was low.

Active

Regulators acknowledging the limits of explainability

EU AI Act enforcement bodies are exploring alternative accountability mechanisms beyond full technical explainability.

Open question

Who governs the model when the model governs itself?

Agentic AI systems that modify their own behaviour create governance challenges no existing framework fully addresses.

Three things to carry forward

Everything else in this session follows from these

01
Ownership is the foundation
Every governance failure traces back to unclear ownership. Until someone is named as accountable, nothing else works.
02
GenAI breaks the governance assumptions
Frameworks built for deterministic, traceable systems don't map cleanly onto probabilistic models. This is the central unsolved challenge.
03
Governance is a discipline, not a checklist
It requires continuous investment, evolves with the technology, and cannot be delegated entirely to a tool or a team.
